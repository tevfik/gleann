# Permafrost Carbon Feedback and Arctic Climate Change

## What is Permafrost?

Permafrost is ground that remains frozen at or below 0°C for at least two consecutive years. It underlies approximately 25% of the Northern Hemisphere's land surface — 22.8 million km² — including vast areas of Siberia, Alaska, northern Canada, and the Tibetan Plateau.

Permafrost soil contains an estimated 1,500 billion tonnes of organic carbon, roughly twice the carbon currently in the atmosphere. This represents the largest terrestrial carbon reservoir on Earth.

## The Active Layer and Talik Formation

The active layer is the surface layer of soil that thaws each summer and refreezes each winter. In recent decades, active layer depth has been increasing across the Arctic due to rising temperatures. Where ground ice melts, the overlying sediment subsides — forming thermokarst landscapes.

A talik is a layer of unfrozen ground within or beneath permafrost that persists year-round. Taliks form beneath deep thermokarst lakes, rivers, and in areas of rapid warming. Once a talik forms, it creates a pathway for microbial decomposition of previously frozen carbon throughout winter.

## Methanogenesis and Carbon Release

As permafrost thaws, previously frozen organic matter becomes accessible to microbial decomposition. Under anaerobic (waterlogged thermokarst) conditions, methanogenic archaea produce methane (CH₄) through methanogenesis. Methane has a global warming potential 28–80 times greater than CO₂ over 100 years.

Under aerobic conditions, aerobic decomposition releases CO₂ directly. The ratio of CH₄ to CO₂ emissions depends on soil hydrology — wet thermokarst produces mostly CH₄, while dry upland permafrost releases primarily CO₂.

## Abrupt Thaw Dynamics

Classical models assumed gradual permafrost thaw from the surface down. However, abrupt thaw mechanisms can release carbon far faster:

- **Thermokarst formation**: Ground ice melt causes collapse and ponding within years
- **Retrogressive thaw slumps**: Hillslope failures exposing deep permafrost
- **Coastal erosion**: 0.5–2 m/year of Arctic coastline retreat

Abrupt thaw could release an additional 125–190 Pg C above gradual thaw projections by 2300.

## Eddy Covariance Flux Measurements

Scientists measure carbon exchange between permafrost ecosystems and the atmosphere using eddy covariance flux towers. These instruments measure vertical wind speed and CO₂/CH₄ concentrations at high frequency (~10 Hz) to estimate net ecosystem exchange (NEE).

FLUXNET-CH4 and the Arctic-Boreal Vulnerability Experiment (ABoVE) compile flux measurements across hundreds of sites. Current measurements suggest many Arctic tundra sites have shifted from carbon sinks to net carbon sources.

## Albedo Feedback and Climate Tipping Points

Permafrost thaw accelerates warming through multiple feedbacks:
1. **Carbon-climate feedback**: Released CH₄/CO₂ drives additional warming
2. **Albedo feedback**: Darker vegetation replaces reflective snow/ice
3. **Shrubification**: Taller shrubs trap more heat
4. **Thermokarst lake albedo**: Open water absorbs more solar radiation

The permafrost carbon feedback is considered a potential climate tipping point. Once sufficient permafrost thaws, the released carbon could push global temperatures above additional thresholds even if human emissions cease.

## Current Scientific Estimates

- Annual permafrost carbon release (current): 0.3–0.6 Pg C/year
- Projected release by 2100 (RCP8.5): 92 ± 17 Pg C
- Fraction of stored carbon released by 2300: ~10% (gradual) to 25% (including abrupt thaw)
- Arctic warming rate: 3–4x faster than global mean temperature
